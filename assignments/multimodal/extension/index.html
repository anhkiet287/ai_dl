Interpretability

Saliency-driven model reading

We use saliency visualizations to understand which image regions drive the classifier's decisions.

Correct case: Dance -> Dance Failure case: Music -> Theater

Case study A: Correct prediction - Dance -> Dance

Zero-shot CLIP

All three methods focus on the dancer's body, pose, and motion silhouette; background receives little attention.

Few-shot Prompt Adaptation

Top-k distribution is dominated by Dance; pose is a strong discriminative factor and the background is ignored.

Few-shot CoOp

Attention is slightly more concentrated on the torso and limbs, but highlights the same main body region.

Case study B: Failure - Music -> Theater

Zero-shot CLIP

Attends to performer, costume, and stage. Nearly deterministic on Theater because cues fit both Music and Theater.

Few-shot Prompt Adaptation

Focuses on the central performer and theatrical set; prediction collapses to Theater because stage cues dominate.

Few-shot CoOp

Less overconfident (about 65%) but still wrong; semantic overlap, not lack of attention, drives the confusion.

Main interpretability takeaway

The models learn meaningful visual regions, but they lean on coarse performance and scene cues. That works for action-centric classes like Dance but is insufficient to separate overlapping editorial categories such as Music and Theater.

Ablation Study

Prompt vs. CoOp vs. zero-shot

Numbers comparing zero-shot CLIP, few-shot prompt adaptation, and few-shot CoOp on the 24-class NYT dataset.

At a glance

  • Prompt adaptation lifts weak classes the most while keeping strong ones stable.
  • CoOp oscillates: helps some (Books, Global Business) but hurts others (Well).
  • Hardest classes persist: Style, Media, Your Money, Well, Economy.
  • Confusions stay local: Movies/Television, Fashion/Style, Music/Theater.

Future work

  • Collect clearer prompts or extra shots for Style, Media, and Your Money.
  • Add disambiguating words for stage vs. concert to split Music and Theater.
  • Calibrate confidence; many wrong predictions exceed 0.95 probability.

Takeaways

  • Prompt adaptation offers the best stability-to-gain trade-off: about +0.16 to +0.15 F1 on the weakest classes (Global Business, Television).
  • Residual errors are mostly local semantic confusions; stronger class wording or contrastive negatives should help.
  • Confidence calibration is needed because many wrong predictions are above 0.95 probability.
